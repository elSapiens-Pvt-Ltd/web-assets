The Red Car Theory
The Reticular Activating System
A bundle of nerves at your brainstem that acts as a gatekeeper — filtering millions of inputs so only what it deems "important" reaches your conscious mind.
🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗 🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗 🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗 🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗
Tell someone to look for red cars, and suddenly they see them everywhere.
The red cars were always there. Your brain was just filtering them out.
That conversation about camping? It primed your RAS to notice camping ads that were already in your feed.
The Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon
Those Ads Were Already There
Named by Stanford linguist Arnold Zwicky — the Frequency Illusion makes something that just caught your attention seem to appear more often. But the frequency hasn't changed. Only your perception has.
1

The Conversation

You talk about camping gear with a friend over coffee.

2

RAS Activates

Your brain now flags "camping" as important. The filter changes.

3

Same Ads, New Eyes

Those camping ads were in your feed yesterday too. You scrolled right past them.

4

Confirmation Loop

Each sighting reinforces the belief: "It's listening!" A self-reinforcing illusion.

The Inconvenient Truth
It's Wrong 99.5% of the Time
You see thousands of ads per day. The vast majority miss completely. But you only notice and remember the hits.
Ads you ignore
~99.5% — invisible to you
Ads that "hit"
0.5%
This is Confirmation Bias
You see 1,000 ads. 997 are irrelevant — your brain doesn't even register them. 3 match something you recently talked about. Those 3 feel shocking.
You remember the 3. You forget the 997. You conclude: "My phone is listening."
Putting It All Together
Three Forces. Zero Microphones.
🧠

The Algorithm

Already knows your interests from behavioral data — scroll patterns, dwell time, social connections, purchase history. No keywords needed.

👁️

Your Brain (RAS)

The conversation primed your attention filter. You now notice things that were already there but previously invisible to you.

🎯

Confirmation Bias

You remember the rare hits and forget the constant misses. The algorithm fails constantly — you only see it when it succeeds.

The truth isn't that your phone is listening to your words.
It's that it doesn't need to.
"They're getting such a deep picture of us as individuals — more so than listening to any conversations."
— Northeastern University researchers, after analyzing 17,000+ apps
The microphone myth is comforting because it implies you can just turn it off.
The reality — that your behavior is the data — is harder to escape.
